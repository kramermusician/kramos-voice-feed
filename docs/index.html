How to Apply to New England Arts Festivals

2026-07-09 · 04:55 · noted

The short version: nobody applies to a summer festival in the summer. The New England cycle opens in the fall and closes in late winter, so a summer 2027 slot is won between roughly October 2026 and March 2027. You have time, and the thing to build in that time is not applications, it is a two-minute video of the projection-plus-looping set actually happening. Every one of these doors opens with a link, not a paragraph.

Where the applications actually live

There is no single New England festival portal. Four channels cover almost everything.

CreativeGround is the regional artist directory run by the New England Foundation for the Arts. It is free, it is where regional programmers look for artists, and it is the single highest-leverage hour you can spend before applying anywhere. Make the profile first.

The state arts councils publish the rolling call lists. Mass Cultural Council posts calls to artists on its ArtSake blog and runs the Festivals grant program on its own cycle. New Hampshire's Arts Council runs an E-Opps page, and Rhode Island's RISCA has an arts-calls page. These are where the small and mid-size festival calls surface first.

Acceptd is the application platform several performing-arts festivals in the region use. Once you have one profile there, subsequent applications are mostly a paste job.

Individual festivals run their own forms. Salem Arts Festival takes performer applications and closed its 2026 round in late March, which tells you the shape of the 2027 round. Somerville PorchFest applications close in the winter for a May festival. Harvard Ed Portal hires stage performers for its Brighton Farmers Market summer series with a March deadline, which is a paid, low-stakes place to test the set locally before you travel with it.

The one that is built for what you are describing

ILLUMINUS is the Boston projection-mapping and light-art festival, running since 2014, recently in Dorchester and Roxbury. It is free to the public, it is explicitly about the intersection of art, design, and technology, and it programs both installations and live performance by artists who manipulate light, sound, and projection at once. It also pairs selected artists with technical mentors on production, which is exactly the gap between what you can do alone and what a projection set needs.

This is not a festival you should apply to as a musician who also has visuals. It is a festival you should apply to as an audiovisual artist. Same set, different framing, and the framing is the whole application.

What a realistic two-festival year looks like

One of the two should be a regional light or new-media festival where the projection layer is the point. ILLUMINUS is the obvious first swing. The other should be a music festival with a side stage or an ambient or late-night programming slot, where a drone and loop set reads as texture rather than as an odd fit. Green River Festival in Greenfield and the MASS MoCA orbit are the right neighborhood for that. FreshGrass is on pause for 2026 and returns to North Adams in September 2027, which is worth noting for the calendar even though its awards program is bluegrass-specific and not your lane.

Aim for one aspirational and one very winnable. Two aspirational applications and a bad summer is a common failure mode.

The street-musician interview thread

This one is stronger than you may realize, because it solves a real problem in the course. LENS-101 asks freshmen what artistic space is and who gets to occupy it. A busker in San Sebastián answering that question on video is a better text than anything assignable. Two practical notes: get a simple release, verbal on camera is enough for classroom use, and record the ambient street audio separately because it is the primary source material for a set about the places you performed. The interviews and the soundscapes are the same project. You just have not been treating them that way.

Next three moves

Build the CreativeGround profile. Film one honest run of the set, one camera, phone audio is fine for now, because nothing else unblocks until that exists. Then in October, when the fall calls post, apply to ILLUMINUS and two others.

Filed as a tracked project, Festival Circuit 2027, with the endpoint: done when you have two confirmed summer 2027 bookings and a rehearsed thirty-to-forty-five minute set.

Zumaia: cliffs, Dragonstone, engines

2026-07-09 · 04:54 · built

Zumaia

A coastal Basque town of about 10,000 people at the mouth of two rivers, the Urola and the Narrondo, sitting between Getaria and Deba on the Camino del Norte. Most walkers pass through it at midday on the Zarautz-to-Deba stage and never learn that they are standing on one of the most important pieces of rock on the planet.

Zumaia is worth slowing down for. It holds three separate stories: a geological record that scientists fly in to read, a beach that stood in for a fictional island, and a family engine company that has been building diesel motors here since 1966.

The cliffs, and why geologists care

The layered rock along the Zumaia coast is called flysch. It forms when a deep marine basin collects alternating layers of hard rock (sandstone or limestone) and soft rock (marl or shale), dropped by underwater avalanches called turbidites. Millions of years of that rhythm stack up like the pages of a book. Then the Pyrenees push the whole book upright, and the sea planes off the edge, and you can walk along the spine and read it.

Each pair of layers is roughly a unit of time. The Zumaia section is continuous, fossil rich, and precisely dated, which is rare. That combination is why the International Commission on Stratigraphy chose it to define global time itself.

Two of the world's official "golden spikes" are driven into these cliffs. A golden spike, formally a Global Boundary Stratotype Section and Point, is the single physical location on Earth that defines the start of a geological age. Everywhere else in the world is dated by comparison to it.

  • The base of the Selandian stage of the Paleocene, ratified in 2008, sits about 49 meters above the dinosaur extinction layer.
  • The base of the Thanetian stage, ratified in 2011, sits about 78 meters above it, along the main beach.

And the extinction layer itself, the Cretaceous-Paleogene boundary that marks the end of the dinosaurs, is exposed at Algorri Cove. It is a thin dark seam in the rock. You can put your finger on the moment.

The whole stretch from Zumaia through Deba to Mutriku is the Basque Coast Geopark, in the European and Global Geoparks Networks since 2010 and UNESCO recognized since 2015.

Itzurun Beach, or Dragonstone

Itzurun is the beach directly beneath those cliffs, a short walk from the town center. Walls of layered rock rise on both sides, roughly 150 meters of vertical strata, which read on camera as something between dragon scales and the ribs of a drowned cathedral.

Game of Thrones filmed here for season seven. This is the beach where Daenerys Targaryen lands at Dragonstone in the season opener, walking up out of the surf onto her ancestral shore. Itzurun returns in episode three, when Jon Snow and Daenerys go into the caves in the cliffs.

Worth keeping straight: the beach is Zumaia, but the castle perched on the rock is a different location entirely, San Juan de Gaztelugatxe, further west toward Bilbao. Two towns, one fictional island. Zumaia gave the show its shoreline.

Above the beach sits the hermitage of San Telmo, a sixteenth century chapel built for the protection of the town's sailors. It appears in the show's establishing shots and it is the best view in Zumaia. Walk up. It takes ten minutes.

The engine factory

Zumaia is not a resort town that happens to have industry. It is an industrial town that happens to have a spectacular coast, and the engine works is the reason.

In 1966 a Biscayan businessman named Jose Maria Gutierrez Ascunce founded Guascor here, to build reliable engines for industry and for ships. The name is a contraction of his own. The company stayed in Zumaia and grew into a serious manufacturer of diesel and gas engines for power generation, cogeneration, waste-to-energy plants, and marine use.

Then it went on a corporate journey. Dresser-Rand, the American industrial group, acquired Guascor in 2011. Siemens bought Dresser-Rand in 2015, and so the Zumaia plant became part of Siemens Energy, and for a while the engines carried the Siemens name with Guascor as a brand underneath. In November 2022 Siemens Energy sold the engine business to Mutares, a German investment holding company, and it was renamed back to what it had always been called locally. Guascor Energy is once again an independent company, still headquartered in Zumaia, still making engines.

There is something fitting about that. A town whose cliffs record sixty million years of deposition, folding, and uplift also holds a company that was founded, absorbed, absorbed again, and then handed back its own name.

Guascor Energy is a working industrial site, not a visitor attraction. There is no factory tour. But if you walk the inland edge of town rather than the coastal path, you will see it, and now you know what it is.

Practical notes for the walk

Zumaia arrives roughly 16 to 18 kilometers into the stage from Zarautz, after the hill route above Getaria. Most walkers treat it as a lunch stop and push on to Deba, another 13 to 14 kilometers with a demanding climb out of town. Some split it into two short days, which is a good idea if the weather is wet.

  • The climb out of Zumaia toward Itziar is steeper than the distance suggests. Do not leave it for late afternoon.
  • Cliff paths get slippery in rain and the coastal junctions are hard to read in fog. Stay on the marked route.
  • Euskotren serves this corridor and is the reliable backstop if weather or a bad knee forces a skip.
  • Small-town kitchens close early. A late arrival in Deba can mean a thin dinner. Eat in Zumaia if you are running behind.
  • Book ahead in summer. Lodging on this stretch is thinner than in Zarautz.

To see the geology properly you want low tide, which exposes the wave-cut platform and lets you walk out onto the layers instead of looking at them from above. Check the tide table the night before. The Basque Coast Geopark runs guided walks and boat trips along the flysch; the tourist office in town has current times.

Six words to carry

Kaixo (hello) · Eskerrik asko (thank you) · Mesedez (please) · Non dago...? (where is...?) · Ur (water) · Zenbat da? (how much is it?)

The History of Legal Sea Foods

_A Massachusetts institution, from a Cambridge fish counter to the presidential table._

The name has nothing to do with lawyers. In the 1940s Harry Berkowitz ran a Cambridge fish market next to the Legal Cash Market grocery, which gave out "Legal Stamps." The fish market borrowed the neighbor's name, and it stuck.

The founding. George Berkowitz opened the first Legal Sea Foods fish market in Inman Square, Cambridge, in 1950 — a retail counter selling fresh fish. In 1968 he opened a restaurant right beside it, serving the same fish at communal tables. The pitch was total freshness: "If it isn't fresh, it isn't Legal."

Growing into an icon. Through the '70s and '80s it expanded across Boston and the Northeast. Roger Berkowitz took over and built the reputation on obsessive freshness, including an in-house lab that tested seafood before that was common. Clam chowder, oysters, and simply grilled local fish became regional touchstones.

The chowder went to Washington. Legal's clam chowder has been served at every U.S. presidential inauguration since Reagan's in 1981 — a Boston recipe turned national symbol of New England cooking.

A Massachusetts story. Family-owned and Boston-headquartered for seventy years, until the Berkowitz family sold the restaurant operations to PPX Hospitality Brands in 2020. What began as one Cambridge fish counter is still one of America's most recognizable seafood names.

Getaria — Camino Cheat Sheet

2026-07-01 · 10:29 · built

Zarautz to Getaria, Stage 2 of the Camino del Norte. Take the hill route above town for the sea views before dropping into the harbor.

The story of Getaria

A tiny harbor town with an outsized history, wrapped around a rocky headland the locals call El Ratón, the Mouse. Juan Sebastián Elcano was born here and in 1522 completed the first circumnavigation of the globe; centuries later Getaria gave the world couturier Cristóbal Balenciaga. Beneath the famous names it's a working fishing town that has grilled its catch over open coals for generations.

Balenciaga Museum, this week

Tomorrow is Thursday, so you're fine — the museum is closed only on Mondays. Standard July/August hours run about 10:00 to 19:00 (usual summer hours, not a same-day confirmation, so check the posted sign for holiday exceptions). A short climb above the town center, easy to fit in or skip.

What to order

Getaria is txakoli country — the crisp, slightly fizzy white poured from a height — plus pintxos and grilled turbot.

  • Pintxo bat hartuko dut — I'll have a pintxo — PEEN-cho baht ar-TOO-koh doot
